Your First Release

The song is finished. The mix is the best you can make it. Now comes the quieter, less romantic work — the work that decides whether anyone outside your room ever hears it. Here is how to release your first track properly, so the music speaks and the machinery stays out of its way.

Releasing music is mostly logistics dressed up as glamour. The artists who seem to "arrive" overnight have usually spent weeks on the unglamorous parts: distribution, metadata, dates, the slow patient work of telling a handful of people who genuinely care. None of it is hard. All of it rewards doing it early, calmly, and once.

Distribution

A distributor is simply the bridge between your file and every platform a listener might open. You upload one master and one piece of artwork, and they deliver it to Spotify, Apple Music, Bandcamp, Deezer and the rest. Choose one that pays out fairly, keeps your rights yours, and does not lock you into anything you would regret in a year. Most charge a small annual fee or a flat per-release cost; avoid anything that quietly claims a slice of your publishing.

Upload your final master as a high-resolution WAV, not an MP3 bounced from your phone. The platform compresses it once; do not compress it twice. Square artwork at 3000 by 3000 pixels, no logos that are not yours, no blurry text. This is the first thing most people will ever see of you — treat it like the cover of a book you are proud of.

Metadata

Metadata is the boring word for everything attached to the song that is not the song: your artist name spelled exactly as you want it forever, the track title, the writers, the producers, the ISRC code, the release date. Get it right the first time. Editing it after release is slow, sometimes impossible, and a misspelled name splits your listeners across two profiles that never speak to each other.

  • Lock your artist name and spelling — capitalisation included — before you upload anything.
  • Credit every writer and producer; split sheets prevent friendships ending over royalties.
  • Register your ISRC and, if you wrote it, your publishing with a collection society.
  • Submit to your distributor at least three to four weeks before release day.
  • Pitch to editorial playlists the moment your release date is set, never after.
  • Keep a private copy of every file, credit and login in one folder you will not lose.

Timing

There is no magic date, but there are sensible ones. Fridays are the industry standard, which means algorithms, charts and listener habits all expect new music then. Give yourself a runway: announce the date, share a fragment, let anticipation do a little of the work. A song released into silence with no warning is a song asking to be missed.

A release is not a launch. It is an invitation. The day the song goes live is not the end of the work — it is the first day people are finally allowed to answer.

Building an audience

You do not need thousands of listeners. You need a few who genuinely care, and a way to reach them again next time. Build the list before you need it: the friends, the early supporters, the strangers who replied to a clip. Tell them personally. Ten messages that land beat ten thousand impressions that scroll past.

Then keep going. One release teaches you almost nothing; three or four teach you everything — what your people respond to, when they listen, what made them share. Treat each one as a quiet experiment and let the data make you wiser, not anxious. The artists who last are the ones who kept arriving long after the first applause faded.
