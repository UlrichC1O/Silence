Own Your Masters

The most expensive lesson in music is rarely about a chord. It is about a contract. You can spend a decade learning to bend a note exactly the way you hear it, then sign away the recording of that note for life in a single warm afternoon — because someone smiled and said the word family.

Wisdom, in this business, is knowing which paper matters before the pen moves. So let us talk plainly about ownership. Not the romance of it — the mechanics. Because the artists who keep their work are almost never the loudest in the room. They are the ones who read the second page.

Two copyrights, not one

Every song you make contains two distinct things, and confusing them is where careers quietly leak money. The first is the composition — the melody, the lyrics, the underlying work. The second is the master — a specific recording of that work. Sell a beat and you may have licensed one while keeping the other. Sign a label deal and you may surrender the master while clinging to the publishing, or, if you are not careful, hand over both.

Owning your masters means owning the recording itself: the file, the asset, the thing that gets streamed, sampled, synced into a film and counted at the end of the quarter. When you own it, the income flows to you and the decisions are yours. When you do not, you are renting access to your own voice.

The shape of a fair deal

A fair agreement is not one with no compromises — it is one where the compromises are named out loud. Watch the term length, the territory, and the reversion: the date the rights come home to you. A deal without a reversion is not a partnership, it is a sale dressed in optimistic language. Watch, too, for who recoups first, and from whose share. An advance of €50,000 feels generous until you learn it is a loan against royalties you have not yet earned.

"They will offer you the world for the thing you cannot make again. Keep the thing. The world comes back around."

Know the words before you sign

Literacy is leverage. These are the terms that decide who keeps the value of a record, long after the session lights go down:

  • Master: the rights in a specific recording — the asset that earns every time it plays.
  • Publishing: the rights in the underlying song, paid through mechanical, performance and sync royalties.
  • Recoupment: the advance a label earns back from your royalties before you see a penny.
  • Reversion: the clause returning your rights to you after an agreed term.
  • Sync: licensing a track to picture — often the single most lucrative decision you will make.

Leverage is patience

The hardest truth is that ownership is cheapest when you need a deal least. Build a catalogue you control, however small. Register your works. Keep your sessions, your stems and your splits documented while everyone still likes one another. The artist who walks in already owning their masters negotiates from a different planet than the one hoping to earn them back.

None of this asks you to become suspicious. It asks you to become informed. Read the page. Ask who owns what, and for how long, and when it returns. Bring a lawyer who answers to you alone. The best gear in the world will help you make the record — only the paperwork decides who keeps it.

That is the whole of our counsel, and it is not glamorous. Own the work. Understand the two copyrights. Insist on a reversion. The wisdom was never in the volume — otherwise, I hear.
